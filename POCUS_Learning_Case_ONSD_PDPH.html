Neuraxial POCUS: Optic Nerve Sheath Diameter and the Post-Dural Puncture Headache

Case developed by Raphael Lessard, MD

Learning objectives

By the end of this case, the learner should be able to:

  • Generate a structured differential diagnosis for postpartum headache and recognize the red-flag features that mandate urgent neuroimaging rather than reassurance.
  • State the major etiologies and approximate incidence of postpartum headache, and describe the clinical features that characterize post-dural puncture headache (PDPH).
  • Explain the anatomical and physiological basis for ultrasonographic optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD) as a non-invasive surrogate for intracranial pressure, and perform the measurement correctly and safely.
  • Interpret ONSD findings in the specific context of intracranial hypotension, and critically appraise the quality and strength of the current evidence for this application.
  • Outline the evidence-based conservative and interventional management of PDPH — including the epidural blood patch — in line with the 2023 multisociety consensus guideline.

This case follows a single patient through a two-part clinical scenario. Work through each question before opening the answer key.

Part I — The Case

Clinical scenario

You are the obstetric anesthesiology resident on the postpartum ward. A 29-year-old G1P1 woman, 24 hours after an uncomplicated spontaneous vaginal delivery at term, asks to be seen for a headache. She had labour epidural analgesia; the chart notes a “technically difficult placement at L3–L4 with one ambiguous loss-of-resistance” before a catheter was sited one interspace higher, with good subsequent analgesia. There was no documented frank cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) return.

She describes a bilateral fronto-occipital headache that began this morning. It is mild when she lies flat but becomes severe within a minute or two of sitting or standing, radiating to the neck. She has associated neck stiffness, mild photophobia, nausea, and intermittent ringing in the ears. She is afebrile, normotensive at 118/74 mmHg, with no focal neurological deficit, no visual field loss, and brisk symmetric reflexes. She is breastfeeding and keen to go home.

Question 1Assess this patient. What is your differential diagnosis for a postpartum headache, and what must you not miss?

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand
  • Postpartum headache is common — it affects roughly one-third to 40% of women in the first week after delivery — and the great majority are benign primary headaches. The clinical task is to identify the minority with a dangerous secondary cause.
  • Approach it as a structured bedside assessment: a focused history (headache onset and tempo, postural character, prior headaches, the neuraxial record, pre-eclampsia features, fever), a neurological and fundoscopic exam, blood pressure, and a deliberate search for red flags.
  • This patient’s postural (orthostatic) headache — worse upright, relieved supine — after a difficult epidural with an ambiguous loss-of-resistance points strongly toward PDPH from intracranial hypotension, but PDPH is a diagnosis of exclusion and the differential must be worked first (Table 1).

Red flags that mandate urgent imaging rather than reassurance

  • Loss of the postural pattern (a previously orthostatic headache becoming constant), focal neurology, seizure, altered consciousness, papilledema, thunderclap onset, fever/meningism, or hypertension with proteinuria/visual symptoms.
  • Any of these moves the workup toward blood pressure/pre-eclampsia evaluation and neuroimaging (MRI/MRV is preferred where available, given CVST and the radiation considerations of CT in a young woman).
Teaching pearlThe single most useful question at the bedside is “what happens to the headache when you sit up?” A crisp postural pattern after neuraxial anesthesia is the hallmark of PDPH; its absence, or its loss over time, should redirect you to the dangerous differential.

Question 2What are the etiologies of postpartum headache, and roughly how common is each? What clinical features define PDPH specifically?

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand
  • Across cohorts, primary headaches dominate (tension-type and migraine together account for roughly half to three-quarters of cases), but secondary causes are over-represented in women referred for evaluation, and these are the ones that carry risk.
  • The frequently-cited distribution below comes from a referral cohort (Stella et al.) and is echoed in subsequent reviews; treat the percentages as order-of-magnitude figures that vary with the population and with referral/selection bias, not as fixed numbers (Table 2).

How common is PDPH after neuraxial anesthesia?

  • Unintentional (accidental) dural puncture complicates roughly 1% (range ~0.4–1.5%) of labour epidurals; when it occurs with an epidural (Tuohy) needle, ~50–80% of those women go on to develop PDPH because of the large-gauge breach.
  • With modern pencil-point (atraumatic) spinal needles the risk after deliberate dural puncture is far lower (often <1–2%). Younger age, female sex, pregnancy, lower BMI, prior PDPH, and larger/cutting needles all increase risk.

Defining clinical features of PDPH

  • Postural headache developing within 5 days of dural puncture — worse within ~15 minutes of sitting/standing, improving within ~15 minutes of lying flat (ICHD-3 / 2023 consensus framing).
  • Typically fronto-occipital, with one or more of: neck stiffness, tinnitus, hypoacusis, photophobia, nausea, and occasionally cranial-nerve signs (CN VI palsy → diplopia; rarely CN VII/VIII) from downward traction on pain-sensitive structures as the brain sags.
  • Pathophysiology is CSF leak → intracranial hypotension, with compensatory venous/meningeal distension and caudal traction generating the pain — the mechanism that ONSD aims to detect.

Question 3Explain the concept of ultrasonographic optic nerve sheath diameter (ONSD). How is it performed, and how reliable and effective is it?

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand

The concept — why the eye reports intracranial pressure

  • The optic nerve is an extension of the brain, wrapped in a dural sheath that is continuous with the intracranial subarachnoid space. CSF circulates within this sheath, so the sheath behaves like a pressure-coupled compartment with the intracranial CSF.
  • When intracranial pressure rises, CSF is forced anteriorly and the sheath distends — the basis for ONSD as a non-invasive marker of raised ICP. The relationship runs the other way too: when intracranial pressure falls (as in a CSF leak / PDPH), the sheath is less distended / smaller. The retrobulbar segment, ~3 mm behind the globe, is the most distensible and the standard place to measure.

How to perform the measurement

  • Patient & preset: supine with the head elevated ~20–30°, eyes closed and looking straight ahead. Use a high-frequency linear probe (>10 MHz) and select an ophthalmic/ocular preset to keep acoustic output low (low mechanical and thermal index, ALARA) — the eye is sensitive to ultrasound energy.
  • Technique: apply a generous layer of gel over the closed upper eyelid; rest the probe lightly — no pressure on the globe — in a transverse orientation until the hypoechoic optic nerve is seen running posteriorly from the globe.
  • Measure: drop a reference line 3 mm posterior to the retina/globe, then measure the sheath width perpendicular to the nerve axis at that point. Repeat in a second (sagittal) plane and in both eyes, and average the measurements to reduce error.
  • Interpretation anchors: normal adult ONSD is roughly ≤5.0–5.7 mm; values above this suggest raised ICP. For intracranial hypotension there is no validated absolute cut-off — the signal of interest is a low ONSD and, more usefully, a decrease from the patient’s own baseline (see Part II).

How effective and reliable is it? Appraise the evidence

  • For raised ICP, the evidence is robust. Meta-analyses report pooled sensitivity ~0.90 and specificity ~0.85 (summary AUC ~0.93) against invasive ICP / imaging references in adults, with similar performance in children. This validates the core premise that ONSD tracks intracranial CSF pressure.
  • For intracranial hypotension / PDPH, the evidence is preliminary and lower-strength. It rests on small single-centre prospective observational studies, a cross-sectional comparison, and case reports — no randomized data, heterogeneous measurement methods, and no agreed threshold. It is best regarded as hypothesis-generating and adjunctive, not diagnostic in isolation.
  • Reliability is operator-dependent. ONSD has a measurable learning curve; inter-observer variability falls with training and with averaging multiple planes/eyes. It is cheap, fast, radiation-free, and repeatable at the bedside — well suited to serial assessment.
Bottom lineONSD is a validated surrogate for intracranial pressure in general, but its use to confirm the intracranial hypotension of PDPH is emerging and unvalidated. PDPH remains a clinical diagnosis; ONSD is a supportive, bedside data point — not a replacement for history, examination, and (when red flags appear) imaging.
Part II — Bedside Ultrasound & Management

Clinical scenario (continued)

You perform ocular POCUS at the bedside. The optic nerve sheath is clearly seen behind each globe. Averaging both eyes in two planes, the ONSD measures ~4.1 mm — at the low end of normal — and when you repeat it after she has been upright for several minutes her symptoms intensify while the sheath appears, if anything, slightly narrower. There is no papilledema and the globes are otherwise normal. The findings are concordant with intracranial hypotension, not raised pressure, and fit the postural history.

Blood pressure remains normal with no proteinuria; the neurological exam is unchanged and non-focal. You make a working diagnosis of PDPH following unintentional dural puncture and discuss management.

Question 4What are the management options for PDPH? Structure your answer using the 2023 multisociety consensus.

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand
  • Management is a stepwise, severity-driven process: set expectations and treat conservatively first, reserve interventional options for headache that is severe or that limits daily function (including caring for a newborn), and keep re-evaluating the diagnosis if the course is atypical.
  • Most PDPH improves with time, but in a postpartum mother “wait it out” is often not acceptable functionally — the threshold to offer definitive treatment is therefore lower than the natural history alone would suggest.

Putting it together for this patient

  • Start conservative therapy now (scheduled acetaminophen + NSAID, hydration, ± caffeine within breastfeeding limits), explain the diagnosis and natural history, and arrange close follow-up with clear return precautions for red flags.
  • Because she is a new mother with a functionally disabling postural headache after a large-bore breach, proactively discuss EBP as the definitive option if she does not settle quickly.

Question 5She elects definitive treatment. Describe the epidural blood patch — indications, technique, efficacy, and risks.

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand
  • Concept: autologous blood injected into the epidural space forms a clot that tamponades the dural breach and acutely raises epidural/CSF pressure, relieving the traction that drives the headache; the clot then promotes sealing of the leak.
  • Indications: established PDPH that is severe or limiting function and not adequately controlled by conservative measures. Confirm the diagnosis and exclude red-flag mimics first.

Technique (key points)

  • Full aseptic technique with two operators: one identifies the epidural space (typically at or one space below the original puncture level), while the second draws ~15–20 mL of the patient’s own venous blood sterilely.
  • Inject blood slowly into the epidural space; stop when the patient reports back/buttock fullness or pressure rather than chasing a fixed volume.
  • Keep the patient supine for a short period afterward and counsel against straining/heavy lifting for ~24–48 h.

Efficacy

  • Provides substantial or complete relief in roughly 65–90% after the first patch; a proportion relapse and a second patch can be performed and is often successful.
  • Lower “cure” rates in the obstetric population reflect the large-gauge Tuohy breach; counsel accordingly.

Risks / complications

  • Common and self-limiting: back/neck ache, transient radicular discomfort, low-grade fever.
  • Procedure-related (shared with any epidural): a further dural puncture, infection, bleeding/neuraxial hematoma, and (rare) neurological injury.
  • Counsel that EBP may complicate future neuraxial procedures in a minority; weigh against the disability of an untreated severe PDPH.
Practice pointInject to symptom/pressure tolerance, not to a target millilitre count. If a patch fails or the headache changes character, re-examine the diagnosis before simply repeating — an evolving or non-postural headache warrants imaging for subdural hematoma or CVST.

Question 6Twelve days later she returns: the headache is now constant, no longer relieved by lying down, and she has new mild drowsiness. What is your concern, and how does the earlier reasoning apply?

Answer keyclick to collapse / expand
  • The alarming change is the loss of the postural pattern together with a new neurological symptom (drowsiness). A PDPH that becomes constant, worsening, or non-postural — especially with focal signs, seizure, or reduced consciousness — is a red flag.
  • The leading concern is an intracranial subdural hematoma: prolonged intracranial hypotension causes the brain to sag and stretches/tears bridging veins. Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (to which pregnancy and the puerperium predispose) must also be excluded, along with other secondary causes.
  • Action: this is no longer a bedside-ultrasound problem — arrange urgent neuroimaging (MRI/MRV or CT/CTV), involve neurology/neurosurgery, and treat the underlying cause. ONSD cannot exclude these diagnoses.

How the earlier reasoning carries through

  • It closes the loop on Q1: PDPH is a diagnosis of exclusion, and the postural pattern is the feature that both supports it and, when lost, refutes it.
  • It reinforces the limits of ONSD from Q3: a non-invasive surrogate is useful for supporting a hypotension picture early, but it does not have the negative predictive value to rule out a structural intracranial catastrophe. Clinical trajectory and imaging govern.
Safety pearlAny dural-puncture headache that changes character — loses its postural quality, becomes constant, or acquires neurological features — is a subdural/CVST until imaging proves otherwise.
