Gastric Ultrasound & the Patient on a GLP-1 Receptor Agonist

Case developed by Raphael Lessard, MD

Domains Preop assessment · Gastric POCUS · Airway/aspiration

This case follows a single patient from the preoperative clinic to an intra-operative complication. Work through each prompt before opening the answer key. The evidence base for GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) in the perioperative period is thin, consensus-driven, and changing quickly — a recurring theme you should be able to articulate to a junior trainee or surgeon by the end of the case.

Learning objectives

  • State the prevalence of GLP-1 RA use and the theoretical mechanism linking these drugs to aspiration risk.
  • List patient- and drug-related risk factors for delayed gastric emptying.
  • Summarize current ASA / multisociety and Canadian (CAS) guidance — and its limitations.
  • Perform and grade a gastric POCUS exam (qualitative + quantitative).
  • Reconcile the discordance between a high-risk gastric grade and the measured risk of clinical aspiration.
  • Manage a peri-induction aspiration event, including the evidence on prophylactic antibiotics.
Part I

Preoperative assessment & risk stratification

Scenario — Part I

A 54-year-old woman is on your morning list for an elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy. BMI 34 kg·m⁻². History: type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 7.1%), hypertension, GERD.

Her medications include semaglutide 1 mg subcutaneously once weekly for diabetes and weight management; her last dose was 3 days ago. No one advised her to hold it. She followed standard fasting instructions.

She feels well and denies nausea, vomiting, or abdominal pain, though she notes mild early satiety that she attributes to the medication. Vital signs are normal. The surgeon asks whether the case "needs to be cancelled."

1
How common is GLP-1 RA use in the surgical population, and what is the theoretical mechanism by which these drugs raise perioperative aspiration concern?
Answer key

Prevalence

  • GLP-1 RAs (semaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide; and dual GIP/GLP-1 agonists e.g. tirzepatide) have grown explosively across diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular/renal risk-reduction indications.
  • Roughly 1 in 8 U.S. adults (~12%) report having used a GLP-1 drug; in surgical claims databases ~4% of elective surgical patients are on one — and rising.
  • Practically: you will encounter these patients on essentially every list, often without it being flagged and without clear hold instructions (as here).

Theoretical mechanism / why we worry

  • GLP-1 RAs mimic incretin: they augment glucose-dependent insulin release, suppress glucagon, increase satiety, and — central to our concern — delay gastric emptying.
  • Delayed emptying means residual gastric content can persist despite adequate fasting, theoretically increasing the risk of regurgitation and pulmonary aspiration on induction/emergence.
  • Aspiration remains the leading cause of anesthesia-related airway death and major morbidity (pneumonitis, ARDS, multi-organ dysfunction); baseline incidence in fasted elective patients is low, roughly 1 in 2,500–4,000.
  • Important nuance: the gastric-emptying effect is most pronounced during initiation/dose-escalation and may attenuate with chronic use (tachyphylaxis) — though how much, and how reliably, is unresolved. Long half-lives (semaglutide ≈ 1 week) mean the effect cannot simply be "held off" the day before.
2
What are the risk factors for delayed gastric emptying / gastroparesis — both GLP-1-specific and general?
Answer key

GLP-1-specific (higher delayed-emptying risk)

  • Dose-escalation phase rather than stable maintenance.
  • Higher dose and weekly (long-acting) > daily/short-acting formulations.
  • Recent dose relative to surgery (long half-life — effect lingers well beyond one day).
  • Active GI symptoms: nausea, vomiting, dyspepsia, abdominal pain, bloating, constipation, or significant early satiety → marker of clinically relevant delayed emptying.

General / patient factors

  • Diabetes mellitus — especially long-standing, poorly controlled (high HbA1c), or with autonomic neuropathy / dysautonomia or retinopathy.
  • Other autonomic / neuromuscular disease: Parkinson's, scleroderma/connective-tissue disease, amyloidosis.
  • Opioids and other anticholinergic/motility-inhibiting drugs.
  • Prior upper-GI / bariatric surgery, vagal injury, gastric outlet obstruction.
  • GERD / hiatal hernia, mechanical obstruction (ileus, bowel obstruction).
  • Metabolic: hypothyroidism, uremia/CKD, electrolyte disturbance; critical illness; pregnancy; eating disorders; idiopathic / post-viral gastroparesis.

Our patient stacks several: diabetes, recent weekly semaglutide, GERD, and early satiety — a reasonable trigger for individualized assessment rather than reflex cancellation.

3
Summarize the current ASA / multisociety and Canadian (CAS) guidance for these patients. What are the limitations of the evidence?
Answer key

ASA 2023 (original consensus guidance)

  • Follow standard ASA fasting; and hold the GLP-1 RA — day of surgery for daily formulations, 1 week before for weekly formulations.
  • Consensus/expert opinion; widely criticized as not following the drugs' pharmacokinetics (a 1-week hold does not clear a drug with a ~1-week half-life), and as causing case delays and cancellations.

2024 Multisociety guidance (ASA-led; with AGA, ASMBS, ISPCOP, SAGES)

  • Explicitly labeled "guidance, not an evidence-based guideline." Pivots from routine holding to a risk-stratified, shared-decision-making model among patient, proceduralist, anesthesia, and prescriber.
  • Most patients can continue their GLP-1 RA before elective surgery.
  • Pre-op screen for elevated delayed-emptying risk (dose-escalation, high dose, weekly formulation, GI symptoms — see Q2). For the highest-risk patients, consider a 24-hour clear-liquid diet before the procedure and/or further measures.
  • If symptoms/risk are not reassuring → manage with full-stomach precautions (RSI, consider gastric POCUS) rather than automatic cancellation.
  • Weigh the harms of holding (glycemic deterioration, cost/resource burden, loss of metabolic/CV benefit). Withholding only for patients with obesity may constitute weight bias and should be avoided. If a hold is chosen, the optimal duration is unknown.

Canada (CAS)

  • CAS issued an early 2023 medication-safety bulletin on semaglutide urging caution; paralleled by an ISMP Canada bulletin and a Can J Anaesth editorial ("proceed with caution").
  • Canadian practice — with strong gastric-POCUS expertise (the Perlas/Toronto grading work) — has broadly moved toward the same individualized, POCUS-informed, shared-decision approach reflected in the 2024 multisociety document, rather than blanket holding. There is no high-quality Canadian RCT or single definitive national guideline superseding this.
Say this out loud The evidence is bleak and moving fast. Every current document is consensus/expert opinion; recommendations reversed within ~18 months (2023 → 2024) and continue to evolve as dual/triple agonists arrive. Defensible practice is individualized risk stratification + full-stomach precautions, not a fixed protocol you can recite.
Part II

Gastric POCUS, the discordance problem & the aspiration event

Scenario — Part II

After shared decision-making you elect to proceed but want objective information first. You bring the ultrasound machine to the bedside to perform a gastric POCUS exam before induction.

The surgeon, watching, says: "If the stomach looks full, that means she's going to aspirate, right? Should we just cancel?"

4
How do you perform and grade a gastric POCUS exam — both the qualitative and quantitative assessment?
Answer key

Acquisition

  • Positioning: scan supine, then right lateral decubitus (RLD). RLD lets dependent fluid pool in the antrum and increases sensitivity. Semi-recumbent is an acceptable alternative; supine alone is least sensitive.
  • Probe / window: low-frequency curvilinear, sagittal epigastric window inferior to xiphoid; identify the antrum between liver and pancreas with aorta/SMA posterior (figure above).

Qualitative assessment — what's in the antrum

  • Empty: flat/collapsed antrum, "bull's-eye"/target appearance.
  • Clear fluid: anechoic/hypoechoic, distended thin walls; transient air bubbles → "starry-night" pattern.
  • Early solid: mixed echogenicity with air, a "frosted-glass" mucosal–air interface along the anterior wall.
  • Late solid: distended antrum with appreciable, heterogeneous particulate content.

Quantitative assessment — how much fluid

  • In RLD, measure antral cross-sectional area (CSA) between peristaltic contractions (average of 3 readings).
  • Validated volume estimate (adults, up to ~500 mL):
Gastric volume (mL) = 27.0 + 14.6 × (RLD CSA in cm²) − 1.28 × age
  • A practical "high-risk" fluid threshold is >1.5 mL·kg⁻¹ (above expected baseline secretions).
  • Any volume estimate is invalid in the presence of solid content — solids are inherently high-risk regardless of calculated volume.

Common pitfalls

  • Mistaking bowel loops for stomach. Bowel typically has thinner walls, lies more superficially, shows active peristalsis/changing content, and lacks the constant landmarks of the antrum. The gastric wall is thicker with its characteristic 5-layered "gut signature."
  • Identifying the stomach but not being at the antrum. Imaging the body/fundus instead of the antrum gives unreliable, non-validated measurements — the volume formula and grading are validated at the antrum only. Confirm the true antral plane by the surrounding landmarks (liver anterior, pancreas posterior, aorta/SMA deep) before measuring.

Scenario — Part II (cont.)

Your exam shows a distended antrum in both positions with heterogeneous, particulate, "frosted-glass" content along the anterior wall — solid material is present. You grade this a Grade 2 / high-risk stomach. The surgeon repeats: "So she will aspirate?"

5
Does a high-risk gastric grade mean a high risk of clinical aspiration? What does the literature actually show across healthy, diabetic, and GLP-1 populations?
Answer key

The short answer

  • No. A "high-risk gastric grade" tells you the stomach is not empty and should change your technique; it is not a measured probability that this patient will clinically aspirate. Surrogate (gastric content) and outcome (aspiration) are discordant in the current data.

Residual-content prevalence climbs steeply

  • Healthy fasted controls: increased/high-risk content in only ~3–8%; inconclusive exams ~3–5%.
  • Diabetes: markedly higher — e.g. "full stomach" in ~48% of T2DM vs 8% of controls in one cohort; meta-analysis confirms diabetes is associated with increased antral CSA, higher residual volume, and more high-risk stomachs (worse with poor control, dysautonomia, retinopathy).
  • GLP-1 RA / semaglutide: increased residual content in roughly 40% vs ~3% of non-users (OR on the order of 25–37 in one propensity-weighted study); "full stomach" ~49% vs 18% of matched controls (OR ≈ 4.3); up to ~70% with solids in fasted volunteers. Solids predominate — the worrisome content type.

...but measured clinical aspiration does not follow

  • Elective surgery, claims database (n ≈ 392,000): aspiration 0.8% in GLP-1 users vs 0.7% in non-users; adjusted OR 1.07 (0.85–1.34), NS.
  • Pooled cohorts: 3 elective-surgery studies (n ≈ 387,000) combined RR 1.00 (0.76–1.30); 4 EGD studies RR 1.10 (0.95–1.27).
  • Endoscopy: aspiration ≈ 4.6 per 10,000 EGDs in GLP-1 users — similar to historical non-user cohorts. Large database studies found no rise in post-op respiratory complications; in diabetics, GLP-1 RAs did not increase EGD aspiration.
  • Positive control: the same methodology detected a real signal for opioids (RR ≈ 2.68) — so the near-null GLP-1 result is unlikely to be pure insensitivity.

An important caveat about that null result

  • The headline aspiration comparisons come from large claims-based / administrative cohorts, which capture real-world practice — not untreated physiology.
  • Many of these patients were almost certainly managed with aspiration risk-mitigation strategies (drug held, clear-liquid diet, gastric POCUS, RSI, cuffed ETT, prokinetics). So an OR/RR near 1.0 may reflect effective mitigation of a real underlying risk rather than the absence of risk.
  • Put differently: the data argue against reflexive cancellation, but they should not be read as "GLP-1 RAs are harmless, ignore the full stomach." The benign outcome statistics likely depend on the very precautions this case teaches. Other limitations: observational design, residual confounding, under-coding of aspiration, and outcomes dominated by fluid rather than solids.
Teaching point Full stomach ≠ aspiration. Most patients with a high-risk grade do not aspirate. Use POCUS to tailor technique (RSI, awake/optimized airway, head-up, suction-ready, delay if elective and feasible), to document shared decision-making, and to de-escalate when the antrum is empty — not as a binary "cancel/aspirate" oracle. Caveats: observational data, residual confounding, under-coding of aspiration, and uncertainty about solid content (the studied outcomes are dominated by fluid).
6
You've confirmed a high-risk (Grade 2, solid) stomach on an elective case. What are your risk-mitigation options — airway choice (ETT vs SGA), induction technique, prokinetics, and pharmacologic prophylaxis — and what does recent evidence say about prokinetics for emptying the stomach pre-op?
Answer key

First decision: proceed, delay, or modify

  • Elective + truly high-risk (solids): the safest option is often to delay/reschedule if clinically reasonable — solids do not clear on the timescale of a short fasting extension, and no maneuver reliably empties a solid-filled stomach.
  • If proceeding, treat as a full stomach and combine the measures below. Regional/neuraxial anesthesia that avoids airway instrumentation is attractive where the surgery allows.

Airway: ETT vs SGA

  • Cuffed endotracheal tube via RSI is the airway of choice. It provides the best available protection against aspiration of regurgitated content.
  • Avoid a supraglottic airway (SGA/LMA) as the primary airway in a known full stomach — it does not protect against aspiration. (A second-generation SGA with a gastric drain port is a rescue/bridge, not a substitute for a definitive airway here.)

Induction technique

  • Rapid-sequence induction: pre-oxygenate, head-up / ramped position, rapid-acting hypnotic + fast-onset neuromuscular blocker (e.g. rocuronium with sugammadex available, or succinylcholine), minimize bag-mask ventilation, suction and skilled assistant ready.
  • Cricoid pressure remains common in RSI though its efficacy is debated — apply per local practice, release if it impairs laryngoscopy or ventilation.
  • Consider gastric decompression with an orogastric/NG tube once the airway is secured (effective for liquid, limited for solids); awake intubation for the rare extreme case.

Pharmacologic acid/volume prophylaxis (does NOT empty solids)

  • Non-particulate antacid (e.g. sodium citrate), H₂-receptor antagonist, and/or PPI raise gastric pH and reduce volume — they mitigate the severity of an aspiration (Mendelson) but do not empty the stomach or remove solids.

Prokinetics — recent evidence (focus on erythromycin)

  • Erythromycin is the most effective gastric-emptying prokinetic. As a motilin-receptor agonist, low/sub-antibiotic doses (~3 mg·kg⁻¹ IV, or ~200–300 mg) induce strong antral contractions and accelerate emptying of both liquids and solids.
  • An RCT in emergency-surgery patients (full stomach) found IV erythromycin increased the proportion with a clear stomach and lowered residual acidity, with minor side effects and greatest benefit in non-trauma patients.
  • Comparative data favor erythromycin over metoclopramide for emptying: a recent guideline review notes erythromycin accelerates emptying whereas metoclopramide, domperidone, and cisapride showed little/no effect in some studies; in critically ill patients erythromycin outperformed metoclopramide. (Some RCTs — e.g. in parturients — show both reduce gastric volume, so metoclopramide is not useless, just generally weaker for emptying and more useful as an antiemetic.)
  • GLP-1–specific evidence is early and thin: a volunteer study showed IV erythromycin counteracts the GLP-1–induced emptying delay, and a published case used serial gastric POCUS to show IV erythromycin 300 mg reduced solid content in a liraglutide patient — but the antrum was not reliably emptied, and optimal dose/timing are unknown. UK (Association of Anaesthetists) and Australasian (ANZCA, 2025) guidance suggests considering a prokinetic while explicitly stating efficacy in GLP-1 RA patients is unproven.
  • Caveats with erythromycin: QT prolongation, CYP3A4 drug interactions, GI cramping, tachyphylaxis with repeated dosing, and acute hyperglycemia attenuates its prokinetic effect (relevant in diabetics). A prokinetic reduces but does not guarantee an empty stomach — re-scan with POCUS and maintain full-stomach precautions regardless.
Bottom line For a confirmed high-risk stomach: delay if you can; if not, RSI + cuffed ETT, acid/volume prophylaxis, and consider IV erythromycin as the prokinetic with the best mechanistic and emerging evidence — then re-scan and still treat as a full stomach. Prokinetics buy probability, not certainty.

Scenario — Part III

This is an add-on that the surgeon is reluctant to postpone, so you proceed with full-stomach precautions: a rapid-sequence induction with a cuffed ETT after pre-oxygenation and ramped positioning, having given acid prophylaxis and considered a prokinetic.

On induction, before the tube is secured, you see particulate gastric material in the oropharynx. After intubation, suctioning the ETT returns small amounts of similar material. SpO₂ briefly dips to 91% then recovers to 97% on 100% O₂; peak pressures are mildly elevated with scattered crackles. A witnessed aspiration has occurred.

7
How do you manage this aspiration event — and would you start prophylactic antibiotics in this otherwise asymptomatic patient who has likely aspirated?
Answer key

Immediate intra-operative management

  • Declare the aspiration and get help; head-down and lateral; suction the oropharynx; apply 100% O₂.
  • Minimize positive-pressure ventilation until the airway is secured; perform RSI / cuffed ETT and suction through the tube before mechanical ventilation.
  • Do not perform routine saline lavage (spreads injury). Reserve bronchoscopy for large-particle airway obstruction, not for clearing acid.
  • Support oxygenation: PEEP, recruitment, lung-protective settings; treat bronchospasm; titrate FiO₂.
  • Assess severity and disposition: a brief desaturation that recovers may be watched in PACU; significant/persistent hypoxemia, infiltrates, or instability → ICU. Decide with the surgeon whether to proceed or abort.
  • Document the event, contents, and management; debrief and disclose to the patient.

Prophylactic antibiotics — the key question

  • No. In a witnessed/likely aspiration of gastric contents in an asymptomatic or early patient, do not start prophylactic antibiotics. The early insult is a chemical pneumonitis, not infection.
  • Evidence: prophylactic antimicrobials after macroaspiration show no mortality benefit, no reduction in ICU transfer, and are associated with more antibiotic escalation and selection of resistant organisms (e.g. Dragan et al., Clin Infect Dis 2018).
  • Most pneumonitis resolves within 24–48 h with supportive care. Steroids are not recommended routinely.
  • When antibiotics ARE warranted: failure to improve / worsening beyond 48 h; clear features of secondary bacterial pneumonia (persistent fever, leukocytosis, new/expanding infiltrate, purulent secretions); or a specific risk for colonized aspirate (small-bowel/distal obstruction, chronic antacid/PPI-associated gastric colonization). Then target likely organisms (often Gram-negatives ± anaerobes) per local antibiogram, and de-escalate / stop if cultures are negative or the patient improves.
Resist the reflex Witnessed aspiration in a stable, asymptomatic patient → observe and support, don't pre-emptively dose antibiotics. Early "just-in-case" antibiotics only breed resistance without preventing pneumonia.

Six takeaways to teach forward

  • GLP-1 RAs are everywhere (~1 in 8 adults) and delay gastric emptying — most relevant during dose escalation, high/weekly dosing, and active GI symptoms.
  • Guidance shifted from "hold everyone" (ASA 2023) to risk-stratified shared decision-making, most continue (multisociety 2024). All of it is consensus opinion and still evolving.
  • Gastric POCUS gives objective, individualized data; grade qualitatively (empty / fluid / solid) and quantitatively (RLD CSA → volume; >1.5 mL·kg⁻¹ or any solid = high-risk).
  • High-risk grades are common in diabetic and GLP-1 patients, but clinical aspiration risk is not correspondingly elevated — use POCUS to tailor technique, not as a cancel/aspirate oracle. The reassuring outcome data are claims-based and likely reflect mitigation already in use.
  • For a confirmed high-risk stomach: delay if feasible, otherwise RSI + cuffed ETT (not an SGA), acid/volume prophylaxis, and consider IV erythromycin (the best-supported prokinetic) — then re-scan and still treat as a full stomach.
  • Manage aspiration supportively; no routine prophylactic antibiotics or steroids — reserve antibiotics for failure to improve at 48 h, superinfection, or colonized-content risk.

Review

End-of-case review

Six questions spanning both parts. Select an answer to reveal the correct choice and a brief explanation.

Review 1 · MechanismWhich best captures GLP-1 receptor agonist prevalence and the mechanism behind the perioperative aspiration concern?

B. ~12% of U.S. adults; the incretin effect delays gastric emptying — most pronounced during initiation/dose-escalation. The long half-life (semaglutide ≈ 1 week) means a one-day hold cannot clear the effect.

Review 2 · Risk factorsWhich patient is at highest risk for clinically relevant delayed gastric emptying?

B. Higher risk with dose-escalation, higher/weekly dosing, a recent dose (long half-life), and active GI symptoms. Diabetes — especially with poor control or dysautonomia — compounds it; our patient stacks several of these.

Review 3 · GuidanceWhich best reflects the shift from the ASA 2023 guidance to the 2024 multisociety guidance?

B. ASA 2023 advised holding (criticized for ignoring pharmacokinetics); the 2024 “guidance, not guideline” moved to individualized risk stratification — most continue, screen for high-risk features, and use full-stomach precautions rather than reflex cancellation. Withholding only for obesity may constitute weight bias.

Review 4 · POCUS gradingOn gastric POCUS, which finding defines a high-risk (“full”) stomach?

C. Grade 2 / high-risk = clear fluid in both supine and RLD, or volume > 1.5 mL·kg⁻¹, or any solid. Measure antral CSA in RLD (GV = 27.0 + 14.6·CSA − 1.28·age); any volume estimate is invalid when solids are present — solids are high-risk regardless.

Review 5 · DiscordanceA diabetic patient on semaglutide has a Grade 2 (high-risk) gastric POCUS. What does this tell you about her risk of clinical aspiration?

B. Residual content is far more common in diabetic/GLP-1 patients (e.g. “full stomach” ~49% vs 18%), yet measured aspiration is not correspondingly elevated (elective surgery adjusted OR ~1.07, NS; opioids were a positive control at RR ~2.68). Use POCUS to tailor technique, not as a cancel/aspirate oracle.

Review 6 · Aspiration eventA witnessed aspiration of particulate gastric content occurs; she stabilizes to SpO₂ 97% with mildly elevated peak pressures and is otherwise asymptomatic. Regarding prophylactic antibiotics:

B. Early aspiration is a chemical pneumonitis, not infection; prophylactic antimicrobials show no mortality or ICU benefit and breed resistance. Support oxygenation, avoid routine lavage and steroids; start antibiotics only for non-improvement at 48 h, secondary bacterial pneumonia, or a colonized aspirate.
