POCUS Learning Case

Perioperative Diaphragm Ultrasound: Diaphragmatic Dysfunction after First Rib Resection

Case developed by Raphael Lessard, MD

Model answers

Learning objectives

By the end of this case, the learner should be able to:

  • Classify the subtypes of thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS) and state the indications for first rib resection.
  • Generate a structured differential diagnosis for respiratory distress following first rib resection, and describe the prevalence and mechanism of phrenic nerve injury in this setting.
  • Perform and interpret point-of-care diaphragm ultrasound, including M-mode excursion and B-mode thickening fraction, using current quantitative cutoffs.
  • Apply a systematic crisis-management approach to acute post-operative respiratory distress in the PACU, integrating lung and diaphragm POCUS.
  • Outline the conservative, surgical, and neurosurgical options for long-term management of confirmed phrenic nerve palsy.
Part I — The Case

Clinical scenario

You are the senior anesthesiology resident covering the PACU. A 31-year-old right-hand-dominant recreational volleyball player has just arrived after an elective right supraclavicular first rib resection with anterior and middle scalenectomy for refractory neurogenic thoracic outlet syndrome affecting her dominant arm. The surgery followed 14 months of failed physiotherapy. A single-shot interscalene-region field block was placed by the surgical team for analgesia. She was extubated uneventfully and is now POD#0, roughly 40 minutes into recovery.

She is anxious, tachypneic at 26/min, using accessory muscles, and reports that breathing feels worse when she lies flat. SpO₂ is 92% on 4 L/min nasal cannula. Air entry is reduced at the right base. She is hemodynamically stable and afebrile. A portable chest radiograph is reported as showing an elevated right hemidiaphragm without obvious pneumothorax.

Question 1What are the subtypes of thoracic outlet syndrome, what causes them, and what are the indications for first rib resection?

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • TOS is compression of the neurovascular bundle as it traverses the thoracic outlet, at three anatomical spaces: the interscalene triangle, the costoclavicular space, and the retropectoralis (subcoracoid) space.
  • It is divided into three subtypes by the structure compressed — neurogenic, venous, and arterial (summarized in Table 1).

Surgical anatomy worth emphasizing for the anesthesiologist

  • The phrenic nerve (C3–C5) runs on the ventral surface of the anterior scalene, coursing lateral-to-medial; the brachial plexus emerges between the anterior and middle scalenes. Both sit directly in the operative field during scalenectomy (Figure 1).
  • The classic decompression is first rib resection with scalenectomy.
  • Common approaches: supraclavicular (best plexus/scalene exposure, used here), transaxillary, and infraclavicular (favored for vTOS).

Question 2Construct a differential diagnosis for her respiratory distress. How common is phrenic nerve injury / diaphragmatic dysfunction in this setting, and why does it occur?

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • Approach this systematically rather than anchoring on the radiograph; the differential spans surgical/pleural complications, the phrenic injury itself, and the usual early post-anesthetic causes (Table 2).
  • Several entries are immediately life-threatening (tension pneumothorax, hemothorax, expanding neck hematoma) and must be excluded first.
  • Key teaching point — two causes in this patient: there are two plausible reasons for an elevated, poorly moving hemidiaphragm — a surgical phrenic nerve injury and the local-anesthetic phrenic block from the field block.
  • They are distinguished largely by time course (the block recovers over hours; surgical injury does not). POCUS cannot separate them at a single time point — serial assessment as the block regresses is key.

Prevalence and mechanism of phrenic injury after first rib resection

  • Overall nerve injury is uncommon — about 1% of TOS decompressions — but the phrenic, long thoracic, and brachial plexus are all at risk because of their proximity to the scalenes and first rib.
  • The supraclavicular approach carries the highest phrenic risk because anterior scalenectomy is performed directly beneath the nerve; reported series describe phrenic complications almost exclusively with this approach, whereas the infraclavicular route for vTOS largely avoids it.
  • Most injuries are neuropraxic — from traction, retraction, or stretch rather than transection — so the majority recover spontaneously over weeks to months; a minority persist and become symptomatic, occasionally requiring plication.
  • Clinically, unilateral diaphragm dysfunction is often well tolerated at rest but symptomatic supine and on exertion — exactly this patient’s pattern of orthopnea and exertional dyspnea.

Question 3How do you assess the diaphragm with POCUS? Describe the views, the measurements, and the quantitative cutoffs you would use.

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • Use two complementary windows (Figure 2): diaphragmatic excursion (how far the dome moves) and diaphragm thickening (how hard the muscle works).
  • Excursion is intuitive but load-dependent; thickening fraction more directly reflects contractile effort and is the more robust marker of intrinsic muscle function.

Probe selection

  • Excursion (subcostal): low-frequency curvilinear probe (2–5 MHz) — provides adequate depth to visualize the dome and its excursion.
  • Thickness (intercostal, zone of apposition): high-frequency linear probe (7–15 MHz) — needed to resolve the thin diaphragm muscle in the zone of apposition.

Window 1 — subcostal / subcostal oblique (diaphragmatic excursion, DE)

  • Patient position: semi-recumbent (30–45°) or supine.
  • Right hemidiaphragm: curvilinear probe in the right mid-clavicular line, subcostally, angled toward the right shoulder, using the liver as an acoustic window. Angle the probe to align the M-mode beam perpendicular to the dome.
  • Left hemidiaphragm: more challenging — use the spleen as the acoustic window in the posterior axillary line; may require lateral decubitus positioning.
  • Switch to M-mode: place the M-mode cursor through the posterior third of the dome, perpendicular to its motion.
  • Measure: amplitude from baseline (end-expiration) to the peak of caudal displacement (end-inspiration), during quiet breathing, deep breathing, and a sniff.

Window 2 — zone of apposition (thickening fraction, DTF)

  • Linear probe at the 8th–9th intercostal space, anterior axillary line, oriented longitudinally (long axis to the ribs).
  • Identify the three-layer structure: pleural line (inner), peritoneal line (outer), and the diaphragm muscle between them.
  • Measure thickness at end-expiration (Tdi-exp) and end-inspiration (Tdi-insp); B-mode or M-mode may be used.
  • On this (operated, right) patient, the right side is examined for dysfunction and compared against the contralateral left side.

Measurements, normal values, and cutoffs

Pattern recognition for paralysis (Figure 3)

  • Paralyzed hemidiaphragm: absent or minimal motion at rest, paradoxical (upward) motion on sniff, and thickening fraction < 20% — frequently with frank thinning rather than thickening during inspiration.
  • The healthy contralateral side is often hyperactive (increased excursion and thickening), a useful internal comparator.
  • Caveat: the sniff test alone is imperfect (~6% of healthy people show some paradoxical motion), so combine excursion and thickening rather than relying on a single sign.
Clinical pearlBoth POCUS techniques have an obvious advantage over the chest radiograph and fluoroscopic sniff test here: they are dynamic, repeatable, radiation-free, and performed at the bedside on an unstable PACU patient. Because the local-anesthetic block confounds the picture early on, serial scans — rather than one snapshot — are what ultimately distinguish a recovering block from a structural phrenic injury.
Part II — Acute Deterioration

Clinical scenario (continued)

Over the next 20 minutes she deteriorates. Respiratory rate climbs to 34/min, she is now unable to lie back at all, visibly diaphoretic and frightened, with paradoxical abdominal motion. SpO₂ falls to 86% despite a non-rebreather mask. Heart rate is 122/min, blood pressure 148/86 mmHg. She can still speak in short phrases. The PACU nurse asks you to take over the airway and “do something now.”

Question 4How do you manage this acutely deteriorating PACU patient? Describe your systematic approach.

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • Priority is a simultaneous resuscitate-while-you-diagnose approach.
  • Call for help early — senior anesthesiologist, the operating surgeon, and PACU support — and work an A–B–C primary survey while immediately optimizing oxygenation and positioning (Table 4).

Decision-making note

  • Intubation is not automatically the answer. If lung POCUS excludes pneumothorax and large effusion and the cause is diaphragmatic, a sitting patient who can protect her airway may be salvaged with NIV/CPAP.
  • Escalate to intubation only if she tires, becomes hypercapnic, or cannot maintain the airway.
  • Remember the earlier interscalene-region block may be contributing and will regress with time.

Question 5How do you use lung and diaphragm POCUS to drive your decision here? What is the significance of unilateral versus bilateral involvement?

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • POCUS is the fastest way to triage this patient at the bedside.
  • Scan the lungs first — to exclude the immediately life-threatening and reversible — then the diaphragm to confirm the mechanism.
  • Lung US (rule out the dangerous first): absent lung sliding with a lung point confirms pneumothorax; a large anechoic space with dependent collapse suggests effusion/hemothorax; diffuse B-lines suggest edema; a consolidation with static air bronchograms suggests atelectasis/aspiration. Lung US substantially outperforms supine chest radiograph for pneumothorax.
  • Diaphragm US (confirm the mechanism): on the operated (right) side expect reduced or paradoxical excursion and a thickening fraction < 20%, with a hyperactive left hemidiaphragm. This confirms hemidiaphragm dysfunction as the driver and argues against an occult intrathoracic catastrophe.
  • Integrate: normal lung sliding bilaterally + paradoxical right diaphragm with TF < 20% = hemidiaphragm dysfunction, not pneumothorax — which supports a trial of NIV and upright positioning rather than a needle or a chest drain.

Unilateral versus bilateral — why it matters

  • This patient has unilateral involvement yet is profoundly symptomatic — a reminder that even unilateral dysfunction can decompensate acutely when superimposed on pain, splinting, residual anesthetic effects, a contributing phrenic block, and the supine position.
  • Reassuringly, this combination is largely supportable and often transient.

Question 6Her diaphragm dysfunction is later confirmed to persist beyond the block. What are the longer-term management options for confirmed phrenic nerve palsy?

Model answerclick to collapse / expand
  • Most post-surgical phrenic injuries are neuropraxic and recover spontaneously, so management is staged: expectant and supportive first, with intervention reserved for symptomatic palsy that fails to recover.
  • Options escalate from conservative measures to surgery (Table 6).

Sequencing principle to take away

  • Observe and support first — spontaneous recovery is the norm.
  • If symptomatic palsy persists, decide between plication and reconstruction at a center that offers both.
  • Generally attempt phrenic nerve reconstruction (functional restoration) before plication (static repositioning) when the patient is a candidate, because plication forecloses later reconstruction.
Clinical pearlTake-home messages. (1) After supraclavicular first rib resection, an elevated, poorly moving hemidiaphragm with orthopnea should prompt diaphragm POCUS. (2) Confirm dysfunction with reduced/paradoxical excursion (deep-breath < 10–11 mm) and thickening fraction < 20%, and always scan the lungs first to exclude pneumothorax. (3) In the PACU, resuscitate while you diagnose, sit the patient up, and reserve intubation for failure of NIV or hypercapnia. (4) An interscalene-region block causes transient phrenic palsy that mimics surgical injury — time and serial scans separate them. (5) Most phrenic injuries recover; plication and phrenic nerve reconstruction are options for persistent symptomatic palsy.
